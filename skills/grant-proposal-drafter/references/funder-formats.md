# Funder formats, logframes and budgets

Quick reference for tailoring drafts. Always defer to a specific call document if one exists — this is for when you don't have one, or want to understand what a funder type expects.

## Common funder types in the African context

**Local & community foundations.** Often shorter, simpler forms. Value local rootedness, community ownership, and realistic budgets. Less bureaucratic language; tell the story clearly.

**Pan-African funds (e.g. TrustAfrica-type, regional pooled funds).** Care about African-led solutions, sustainability, and systemic change. Emphasise local leadership and partnership.

**Bilateral / multilateral donors (EU, UN agencies, FCDO, USAID-type, GIZ, etc.).** Heaviest formats. Expect a logframe or results framework, detailed budgets, risk matrices, theory of change, M&E plans, and value-for-money justification. Strict compliance — follow their template to the letter. Eligibility and co-funding rules are often firm.

**INGO sub-grants.** The INGO passes on donor requirements; mirror their format. Relationship and alignment with the INGO's program matter.

**Corporate / CSR & foundations.** Often theme-specific (education, health, environment, women/youth). Value clear, brandable impact and reporting. Shorter than institutional donors.

**Crowdfunding / individual donors.** Story-first, emotional but dignified, concrete asks ("$50 trains one teacher"). Different register entirely from institutional proposals.

## The logframe / results chain

Most institutional funders want to see a clean results chain. Even when not required, thinking this way sharpens any proposal:

```
INPUTS  →  ACTIVITIES  →  OUTPUTS  →  OUTCOMES  →  IMPACT
(money,    (what you     (immediate  (changes in    (long-term
 staff)     do)           products)   behaviour/      change)
                                       conditions)
```

- **Output** example: "200 community health workers trained."
- **Outcome** example: "Trained workers correctly diagnose and refer malaria cases (measured by spot-checks)."
- **Impact** example: "Reduced under-5 malaria mortality in the district."

Each level needs at least one **indicator** (how you'll measure it), a **baseline**, a **target**, and a **means of verification** (where the data comes from). If the NGO doesn't have baselines, note that a baseline survey is part of the project rather than inventing figures.

## Theory of Change (one line)
A Theory of Change explains *why* you believe the activities will lead to the impact — the assumptions in between. Funders increasingly ask for it. Keep it as: "If we do X, then Y will change, because [assumption], leading to [impact]."

## Budget basics
Typical budget categories:
- Personnel / staff
- Activities / direct program costs
- Equipment & supplies
- Travel & logistics
- Monitoring, Evaluation & Learning (M&E) — funders notice when this is missing
- Administration / indirect / overhead (often capped, e.g. 7–15%; check the funder's rule)
- Co-funding / in-kind contributions (if required or available)

Sanity checks to run on any budget:
- Does every budget line trace to an activity in the narrative?
- Is M&E actually funded (not zero)?
- Is overhead within the funder's allowed cap?
- Are figures realistic for the context, not copy-pasted from a Western template?
- Does the total match the funder's ceiling and the amount stated in the narrative?

Never invent specific monetary figures and present them as the NGO's real costs. Provide structure and placeholders; the NGO confirms the numbers.
