# Indicator quality, baselines, targets and means of verification

How to choose indicators a lean African NGO can actually measure, and how to set baselines, targets and verification methods honestly.

## 1. What an indicator is (and isn't)

An **indicator** is a specific, observable sign that tells you whether a result is happening. It is not the result itself — it's the evidence you'll point to.

- Result (outcome): "Girls re-enrol in formal school."
- Indicator: "% of programme participants enrolled in formal school 12 months after completing the catch-up course."

Each indicator needs four things to be usable: a **definition** (what exactly is counted), a **baseline** (the starting value), a **target** (the value you're aiming for), and a **means of verification** (where the number comes from). An indicator missing any of these is not yet finished.

## 2. Quality criteria for indicators

A practical, lean-team version of the usual SMART/CREAM criteria:

- **Specific** — one clear thing, defined precisely. "% of participants who can read a paragraph aloud" beats "literacy improved".
- **Measurable with what you have** — be honest about the team and tools. If measuring it needs a survey the team can't run, it's the wrong indicator.
- **Attributable** — plausibly connected to the project's work, so a change in the indicator says something about the project.
- **Relevant** — it measures the *result*, not just activity. Prefer one good outcome indicator over five output counts.
- **Time-bound** — it specifies when it's measured (endline, quarterly, 12 months after).
- **Affordable** — collecting it doesn't cost more than the insight is worth.

A good test: "If this number moved, would we actually believe something real had changed for the people we serve?" If not, it's probably a vanity metric.

## 3. Vanity-metric traps to avoid

These are easy to count and weak as evidence. Flag them when you see them dominating a framework:

- **Counting attendance as impact** — "500 people attended the workshop" says nothing about whether anything changed.
- **Counting outputs only** — pages printed, sessions held, posts shared. Necessary for monitoring, but not evidence of outcomes.
- **Reach without depth** — "reached 10,000 people" with no measure of what those people did or gained.
- **Self-reported satisfaction as the only outcome** — useful, but thin if it's the sole evidence of change.
- **Cumulative numbers that only ever go up** — designed to look impressive, hard to interpret.

The fix is usually to pair an output count with one honest outcome indicator: not just "200 trained" but "of 200 trained, X% demonstrably applying the skill at follow-up".

## 4. Baselines

A **baseline** is the value of the indicator before the project starts (or at its very beginning). Without it, targets and results are uninterpretable.

- If the NGO has baseline data, use it and note its source.
- If they don't (common for grassroots orgs), **do not invent one.** Recommend a short baseline survey or entry assessment as the first project activity, and mark the baseline `[TO CONFIRM — baseline survey]`.
- Baselines can sometimes come from existing records: county education statistics, clinic registers, prior project data. Suggest these where realistic, but flag that they need verification.

## 5. Targets

A **target** is the value you commit to reaching by a stated point. Targets must be:

- **Grounded in the baseline** — "increase from 40% to 60%" is meaningful; "increase by 20 percentage points" floating with no baseline is not.
- **Realistic for the context and budget** — an over-ambitious target set to impress a donor becomes a failure on the evaluation report. Better to commit to what's achievable.
- **The NGO's to set, not yours.** Propose a sensible range and the reasoning, but mark the final figure `[TO CONFIRM]` for the NGO to own.

## 6. Means of verification

The **means of verification (MoV)** is the specific source of the data for each indicator: attendance registers, enrolment records, a follow-up phone survey, clinic registers, photographs, case files, a third-party assessment.

The test for a good MoV is brutal but simple: **will this data source still exist and be maintained six months from now, by this team?** A verification method that depends on a system the NGO doesn't have is a fiction. Favour sources already part of the work (registers, records) over new ones that need building, and where a new tool is needed, name it as a project cost.

## 7. Disaggregation

Most donors and most honest analysis require results broken down by relevant groups — typically **sex/gender, age**, and often **disability, location (rural/urban), or other equity dimensions** relevant to the project.

- Decide disaggregation at design time — it's painful to add later.
- Only collect disaggregation you'll actually use and that you can gather safely. Collecting sensitive categories (disability, HIV status, ethnicity) carries data-protection and dignity obligations — gather them only with consent and a clear purpose, and store them aggregated.
