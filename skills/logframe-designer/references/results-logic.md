# Results logic, the results chain, and theory of change

A working reference for designing the logic of a project. Always defer to a specific donor's logframe template if one exists — this is for when you don't have one, or want the logic to hold up regardless of format.

## 1. The results chain

Most institutional funders and most good M&E systems use the same ladder of results:

```
INPUTS  →  ACTIVITIES  →  OUTPUTS  →  OUTCOMES  →  IMPACT
(money,    (what you      (immediate  (changes in     (long-term
 staff,     do with         products    behaviour,       change in
 time)      the inputs)     of the      practice or      lives /
                            activities) conditions)      conditions)
```

- **Inputs** — the resources: funding, staff, vehicles, training materials, time.
- **Activities** — what the project does: train, distribute, mentor, build, convene.
- **Outputs** — the direct, countable products of activities. Almost fully within the NGO's control. *"200 community health workers trained."*
- **Outcomes** — the changes that result when outputs are used. Partly outside the NGO's control. *"Trained workers correctly diagnose and refer malaria cases."*
- **Impact** — the long-term, higher-level change the project contributes to (rarely caused by it alone). *"Reduced under-5 malaria mortality in the district."*

Different donor templates rename these (goal / purpose / result / output), but the ladder is the same. Map the NGO's project onto this ladder before worrying about anyone's column headings.

## 2. Telling the levels apart (the hard part)

The single most common logframe mistake is collapsing **outputs** and **outcomes**. Use these tests:

- An **output** is something you can guarantee if you do the activity. "Girls attend catch-up classes" is an output — you ran the classes, they came.
- An **outcome** is a change in someone's life, behaviour or situation that the output is *meant to cause* but cannot guarantee. "Girls re-enrol in formal school" is an outcome — it depends on the girls, their families, the schools.

A quick rule: if it's a thing the NGO *delivered*, it's an output. If it's a change in the *people or system* the NGO serves, it's an outcome. Donors fund outcomes; weak frameworks promise outputs and call them impact.

| Level | Whose behaviour/state changes | Example (girls' education) |
|---|---|---|
| Output | The project's delivery | 120 girls complete a 6-month catch-up course |
| Outcome | The beneficiaries | 70% of those girls re-enrol in formal school within a year |
| Impact | The wider community | More young women in the county complete secondary education |

## 3. Theory of change — the pattern

A theory of change (ToC) explains *why* you believe the activities will lead to the impact — it makes the assumptions in the gaps visible. Build it before the matrix; it catches broken logic early.

The one-line pattern:

> **If** we [do these activities], **then** [this will change], **because** [these assumptions hold], **leading to** [this impact].

A fuller ToC works backwards from the impact:

1. State the long-term **impact** you want to contribute to.
2. Ask: what **outcomes** would have to be true for that impact to happen?
3. Ask: what **outputs** would produce those outcomes?
4. Ask: what **activities** produce those outputs?
5. At each "then", name the **assumption** that has to hold for the arrow to work.

**Worked example (smallholder agriculture, northern Ghana):**

> *If* we train women farmers in drought-tolerant seed and provide access to it, *then* they will plant it and harvest more reliably, *because* the seed suits local rainfall and markets will buy the surplus, *leading to* more stable household income and food security across the season.

The assumptions ("the seed suits local rainfall", "markets will buy the surplus") are exactly where projects fail — naming them is the point.

## 4. Assumptions and risks

An **assumption** is an external condition that must hold for a step in the chain to work, but which the project does not control. A **risk** is the same thing stated as what could go wrong.

For each link in the chain, ask: "What has to be true outside our control for this arrow to hold?" Examples: rains arrive on time; schools stay open; community leaders keep referring participants; the currency doesn't collapse the budget; security allows field access.

Institutional donors require an assumptions column precisely because honest assumptions show the NGO understands its operating reality. Don't leave it blank, and don't fill it with trivialities.

## 5. Common logic failures to flag

When reviewing a framework (the NGO's or your own draft), call out:

- **Orphan activities** — an activity that doesn't lead to any stated output or outcome. Either it's not needed, or an outcome is missing.
- **Output-as-outcome** — an "outcome" that is really just a delivered output (see section 2).
- **Heroic leaps** — an outcome that depends on a huge, unstated assumption (e.g. "training leads to regional policy change") with nothing in between.
- **Unmeasurable impact promised as a target** — impact-level change the NGO can't plausibly measure within the project, presented as a deliverable.
- **No assumptions named** — usually a sign the logic hasn't been stress-tested.
- **Targets with no baseline** — a target like "increase by 30%" is meaningless without knowing the starting point. Flag for a baseline survey.
