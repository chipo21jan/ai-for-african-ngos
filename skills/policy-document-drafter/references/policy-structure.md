# Policy structure

The standard bones of a good NGO policy, plus how the shape changes for different document types. A recognisable structure is part of what makes a policy credible to a funder or registrar — they expect to see these elements and can tell a serious document from a copied one.

**Jurisdiction note.** Structure is fairly universal, but the *content* of several sections (legal definitions, roles required by law, the binding effect of an agreement, mandatory clauses) depends on the organisation's country. Mark anything legal as `[TO CONFIRM]` and prompt a local-law check before the document is relied on.

## The standard structure of a good NGO policy

1. **Title and document control** — policy name, version number, approval date, review date, and the owner (the position responsible). Put this at the top so anyone can see at a glance whether the policy is current and who maintains it.
2. **Purpose** — why this policy exists, in one or two sentences. What it's trying to achieve or prevent.
3. **Scope** — who and what the policy applies to: which staff, volunteers, board members, contractors, partners; which activities or locations. Be explicit about the edges.
4. **Definitions** — plain-language meanings of key terms used in the policy, so it can't be misread. Keep these grounded in local law where the term is legal `[TO CONFIRM]`.
5. **Policy statement** — the organisation's actual position. The commitments it makes and the standards it holds itself to. This is the heart of the document.
6. **Roles and responsibilities** — who does what, named by *position* not individual. Who approves, who implements, who is the point of contact. Vagueness here is where policies fail.
7. **Procedures** — the practical "how": the steps people actually follow. This is what turns a statement of intent into something usable day to day.
8. **Related policies** — cross-references to other documents (a code of conduct, an HR policy, a data-protection policy) so the set hangs together.
9. **Review date** — when the policy will next be reviewed (at least annually for most, and after any serious incident for safeguarding).
10. **Version control** — a short history of changes, so it's clear which version is live.
11. **Board approval line** — a line confirming the governing body adopted the policy, with the date and the signatory's position. Without this, the policy hasn't formally taken effect.

## How the shape changes by document type

### HR / staff policy
The most procedure-heavy document, and the most jurisdiction-dependent. Beyond the standard structure it typically covers: recruitment, contracts and probation, working hours and leave, remuneration, conduct and discipline, grievances, and exit. Notice periods, leave entitlements, dismissal procedures and discrimination protections are set by **employment law that differs sharply by country** — flag every one as `[TO CONFIRM with local employment law]`. Because it handles sensitive personal data, it should align with the country's data-protection law (POPIA, Kenya DPA, Nigeria NDPA, etc.).

### Code of conduct
Shorter and behaviour-focused. Less procedure, more clear statements of expected and prohibited behaviour, with consequences for breaches. Often cross-references the safeguarding policy, the HR disciplinary procedure and any conflict-of-interest rules. The value is in being concrete and unambiguous, so people know exactly where the lines are.

### Memorandum of Understanding (MoU)
A different animal — it's an agreement between two or more parties, not an internal policy. Structure: the parties, the purpose of the partnership, each party's roles and contributions, duration, how it's governed and communicated, handling of money/assets/IP if relevant, confidentiality and data sharing `[TO CONFIRM data-protection obligations]`, dispute resolution, how it can be amended or ended, and a signature block for each party. **Whether an MoU is legally binding or merely a statement of intent depends on its wording and the jurisdiction** — this is a point to flag clearly for a local lawyer.

### Board minutes template
A record, not a policy. A reusable template captures: organisation name, meeting date/time/place, attendees and apologies, confirmation of quorum, approval of the previous minutes, agenda items with decisions and who is responsible for follow-up actions (and by when), any conflicts of interest declared, and the date of the next meeting. The point is a consistent, defensible record of decisions — useful for governance, audits and funder due diligence.
