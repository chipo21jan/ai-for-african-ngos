# Safeguarding essentials

What a credible safeguarding / child-protection policy must cover. This document exists to keep children and at-risk adults safe — every section below is judged against that, not against how well it protects the organisation. Use this as a completeness check when drafting, alongside `../assets/safeguarding-policy-template.md`.

**A note before you start.** Child-protection law and mandatory reporting duties vary by country — who you must report abuse to, within what time, and what counts as a child or a vulnerable adult are all defined differently in South Africa, Kenya, Nigeria, Uganda, Ghana and elsewhere. Treat every legal point below as `[TO CONFIRM]` for the organisation's jurisdiction, and prompt them to have the final policy checked by someone who knows local child-protection law.

## What the policy must cover

### 1. Scope and who it protects
State clearly who the policy is designed to protect — children (define the age, as it varies by country), and at-risk or vulnerable adults — and who it binds: staff, volunteers, board members, contractors, consultants, partners and anyone acting in the organisation's name. Safeguarding fails at the edges, so the scope should be wide and explicit.

### 2. Definitions of abuse and harm
Plain-language definitions of the forms of harm the policy addresses: physical abuse, emotional/psychological abuse, sexual abuse and exploitation, neglect, and — for organisations handling money or aid — financial exploitation and abuse of power. Include online and remote harms where relevant. Definitions matter because staff can only report what they can recognise.

### 3. Code of conduct for staff and representatives
The behaviours expected of everyone acting for the organisation, and the behaviours that are never acceptable (for example: being alone with a child without justification, any sexual contact with a beneficiary, sharing images of children without consent). This is the day-to-day spine of safeguarding — it tells people exactly what is and isn't allowed. A standalone code of conduct can cross-reference this.

### 4. Safe recruitment and vetting
How the organisation reduces the risk of bringing harmful people in: advertised expectations, interview questions on safeguarding, reference checks, and criminal-record/background vetting where it is available and lawful in the country `[TO CONFIRM what vetting is legally available locally]`. Be realistic about what a small organisation can actually do, and say what it will do rather than promising a system it can't run.

### 5. Reporting procedures and whistleblowing
The single most important operational section. How anyone — staff, volunteer, community member, child — raises a concern: who they tell, how, and what happens next. Provide more than one route (so a concern about a manager doesn't have to go through that manager), protect anyone who reports in good faith from retaliation, and make the route accessible (consider language, literacy, and reaching children directly). Include a whistleblowing route for wrongdoing more broadly.

### 6. Response and referral
What the organisation does once a concern is raised: who assesses it, immediate steps to keep the person safe, when and to whom it is referred externally (police, child-protection authority, social services — all `[TO CONFIRM]` for the jurisdiction and its mandatory-reporting duties), confidentiality limits, and record-keeping. The response must prioritise the safety of the person at risk, not the comfort of the organisation.

### 7. Survivor- and child-centred principles
A short, explicit statement that the safety, wishes and dignity of the survivor or child come first throughout, that they are listened to and not blamed, and that support is offered. This is the ethical core; it should be stated, not left implied.

### 8. Designated safeguarding lead
Name the *position* (not a person) responsible for safeguarding — the point of contact for concerns, the person who manages referrals and keeps records, with a named deputy so there's always cover. A policy with no clear owner doesn't get implemented.

### 9. Training and awareness
How staff, volunteers and the board are made aware of the policy and trained to recognise and report concerns — on induction and refreshed periodically. A policy no one has been trained on protects no one.

### 10. Monitoring and review
A stated review cycle (at least annually, and after any serious incident), who is responsible for reviewing it, and how lessons from incidents feed back in. Include a version line and the board-approval date so it's clear the policy is live and current.

## Quick completeness check
A safeguarding policy that's missing any of these is not yet credible to a serious funder:
- [ ] Clear scope — who it protects and who it binds
- [ ] Definitions of the forms of abuse/harm
- [ ] Code of conduct (expected and prohibited behaviours)
- [ ] Safe-recruitment and vetting steps (realistic for the org's size)
- [ ] Reporting routes — more than one, accessible, retaliation-protected
- [ ] Response and referral, with external authorities `[TO CONFIRM]`
- [ ] Survivor-/child-centred principles stated explicitly
- [ ] Named designated safeguarding lead (position) and deputy
- [ ] Training plan
- [ ] Review cycle, version line and board-approval date
