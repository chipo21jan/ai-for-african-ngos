# Policy intake checklist

Gather this before drafting. Ask in grouped batches, not one question at a time. Anything unknown becomes a `[TO CONFIRM]` placeholder rather than an invented answer — this is especially important for legal facts, registration details and reporting duties.

## A. Which document
- Which document is needed? (safeguarding / child-protection policy, HR / staff policy, code of conduct, MoU, board minutes template, or something else)
- Is this a brand-new policy, or are they revising or strengthening one they already have?
- Did a funder, registrar or auditor ask for it, and did they specify a format or required contents to mirror?

## B. The organisation and who it covers
- Legal name and any operating/brand name
- Country (and any other countries they operate in) — this determines which law applies
- What the organisation does, in a line or two
- Who the policy must cover: staff, volunteers, board/trustees, contractors, consultants, partners, the community/beneficiaries they serve
- Rough size: number of staff and volunteers (a four-person team and a forty-person team need different procedures)
- Registration type and number, if relevant `[TO CONFIRM]`

## C. Their real current practice
- What do they already do in this area, even informally? (e.g. how concerns are currently raised, how they recruit, how leave is handled)
- Is there any existing written rule, handbook or agreement to build on?
- What do they genuinely intend to do going forward, versus what's aspirational? (The policy must describe real, deliverable practice — not a fantasy version of the organisation.)
- Who currently holds key roles the policy will name (e.g. who would be the safeguarding lead, who approves leave, who signs agreements)?

## D. Jurisdiction and external requirements
- Which country's law governs the organisation and its staff?
- Any specific funder or registrar requirement to mirror (their template, their mandatory clauses, their definitions)?
- For HR or safeguarding: are they aware of any local legal duties that must be reflected (mandatory reporting, notice periods, leave entitlements)? Mark these `[TO CONFIRM with a local lawyer / the relevant authority]`.
- For an MoU: who are the other parties, and what is the partnership for?

## E. Language and approval
- What language(s) must the policy be available in for the people it covers to actually understand it?
- Who in the organisation approves and adopts the policy (the board, a director, a governing committee)?
- Who will own and maintain it after adoption, and how often will it be reviewed?

## Quick-start (minimum viable intake)
If the user is in a hurry, the four things you most need are: (1) which document they want, (2) their country, (3) who the policy covers and how the organisation actually works in that area today, and (4) who has to approve it. From these you can produce a useful first draft and flag everything else — registration numbers, exact legal duties, named roles — as `[TO CONFIRM]` gaps, with a clear prompt that a qualified local lawyer should review the result.
